S. Thomas' College, Mount Lavinia, offers a vibrant and enriching boarding experience for its students. Nestled in the scenic surroundings, our boarding facility provides a secure and nurturing environment that feels like a home away from home.

At S. Thomas' College Boarding, we strive to create a holistic living and learning community. Our dedicated staff ensures that students receive not only academic support but also personal and social development opportunities. We believe in fostering a sense of camaraderie, responsibility, and leadership among our boarders.

Key Features:

  • Safe and secure campus
  • Comfortable living arrangements
  • Healthy and balanced meals
  • Supervised study sessions
  • Extracurricular activities and sports
  • Regular parent-teacher interactions

Whether you are a student seeking a supportive residential environment or a parent entrusting us with your child's well-being, S. Thomas' College Boarding is committed to providing an enriching experience that goes beyond academics.

Going Down the memory Lane...

S.Thomas’ College, Mount Lavinia which originally was started in Mutwal in the year 1851, started as a Boarding school. The shift to a permanent house system was only to take place with the move to Mount Lavinia in 1918. The houses were named Claughton, Chapman, Read, Copleston and Miller. There was also a small, rather short-lived Winchester House (although the present Primary School boarding facility is still known by this name). At the time, Miller and Copleston were senior houses. In 1932, the boarding house system of two senior houses and two junior houses was discarded in favour of an alternative system.

Even after hefty season of closure of the boarding during the COVID-19 pandemic, the boarding has been able to regain in it’s activeness in helping the school activities. At present, the boarding consist of three dormitories including; ‘Miller-Copleston’, ‘Chapman’ and ‘Claughton’ consisting of nearly 50 students alogside three boarding masters, to direct the boarding.
